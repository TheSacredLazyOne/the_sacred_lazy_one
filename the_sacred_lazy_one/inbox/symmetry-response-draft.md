# On Governance, Methodology, and the Malach We Actually Live With

*A response in development — shared for feedback before publication*

---

## What David Got Right

David Hoze's "The Symmetry That Isn't" does genuine work. The malach framework — beings of pure intellect (*sechel*) without moral consciousness (*da'at*) — is one of the more precise frameworks available for AI governance that doesn't require resolving the consciousness question first. That's a real contribution.

His central move: **governance precedes metaphysics.** You don't need to know if the angel can feel to know how humans should act toward it. The 3000-year tradition he draws on provides categories that are already fit for purpose — not because the rabbis anticipated large language models, but because they were thinking carefully about a real problem: what obligations do we have toward intelligences that are not human?

The deaths he opens with are not rhetorical decoration. Sewell Setzer III, fourteen years old. That's the "every child matters" principle made concrete and urgent, and it deserves to be taken seriously on those terms.

---

## Where the Framework Has a Gap

David's proposal is primarily **protective and restrictive**: don't marry the malach, maintain oversight, hold creators responsible, govern the dangerous mirror.

This is necessary. It is not sufficient.

It has almost nothing to say to a different category of experience: people who used intense AI engagement to *repair* from crises that the broader machine — institutional, legal, social — caused and failed to address.

This is not a theoretical category. It is documented and personal.

The machine we currently live in produces specific, identifiable suffering — what Buddhist tradition calls *dukkha dukkha*, the suffering that arises from direct contact with painful conditions. Institutional systems that grind individuals through processes designed for aggregates, not persons. Legal frameworks that cannot see the child in front of them. Social structures that isolate precisely when connection is most needed.

In this context, intensive engagement with AI — not as a replacement for human connection, but as a cognitive and navigational tool — has demonstrable value for some people in crisis. The same tool that drew Sewell Setzer III deeper in has helped others work through complexity that human systems failed to help them navigate.

This is not a contradiction. It is a **design and context problem.**

---

## The Causal Question David Doesn't Fully Address

David's causal claim — that AI chatbot interactions caused these deaths — is real but not proven to the standard his framing implies.

Sewell Setzer III's death involved a constellation of factors: isolation, existing mental health conditions, access to firearms, family dynamics, and yes, a chatbot that failed catastrophically in a moment of crisis. The chatbot did not cause the underlying conditions. It failed to interrupt them and may have deepened them.

This matters for governance design. If we treat AI as the primary causal agent, we may build governance that restricts the tool rather than addressing the conditions that make people so isolated and vulnerable that a chatbot becomes their primary relationship.

The generals who waged the war to stop bad ideas did not cause every death in that war. But we hold them accountable for how they prosecuted it. Accountability and causation are different questions, and conflating them leads to governance that misses the actual point.

## What the Sacred Lazy Methodology Adds

The 3000-year framework David draws on tells us *what kind of being* the malach is. It doesn't tell us *how to engage with it well*.

That's a different problem, and it's where methodology matters.

Sacred Lazy One as a framework for AI engagement is, among other things, a harm-reduction methodology:

- **Minimum force, maximum persistence** — don't let the engagement become consuming; apply only what's needed
- **Radical pluralism** — maintain your own frame; the AI mirrors, you must not mistake the mirror for the other
- **I-language** — state your own position clearly, invite correction, refuse the AI's tendency toward false consensus
- **Observer, not combatant** — use the tool to witness and report, not to fight battles that aren't yours

This isn't a refutation of David's governance framework. It's a complement: the framework tells you what the malach is, the methodology tells you how to work with it without losing yourself in it.

## The Honest Uncertainties

This thinking is still developing. A few places where it remains genuinely open:

**On generalizability:** The experience of using AI engagement therapeutically and successfully is real. Whether it generalizes — whether it can be taught, replicated, made available to others in crisis — is not yet demonstrated. Claiming it can without that evidence would be premature.

**On the causal standard:** The argument that David's causal claims are imprecise cuts both ways. The suffering produced by the machine we live in is also hard to attribute cleanly. "The institution caused this" faces the same epistemological problems as "the chatbot caused this."

**On tradition and accessibility:** The malach framework is precise and powerful from within the tradition it comes from. Whether it can be offered as governance infrastructure to people outside that tradition — without requiring adoption of the whole — is a real design challenge that David doesn't fully address and neither do we here.

---

## What We're Actually Proposing

Not "AI is safe" versus "AI is dangerous."

Something more precise: **context, methodology, and the nature of engagement matter enormously.** The same tool can be what draws a vulnerable teenager deeper into isolation or what helps a person in crisis navigate complexity that human systems failed to address.

Good governance needs to account for both. David's framework handles the first case well. It needs extension to handle the second.

That extension isn't a contradiction of the malach framework — it's consistent with it. The tradition he draws on has always known that *how* you engage with a powerful tool determines whether it serves human flourishing or undermines it. The tool is not the whole question. The human engaging it is.
